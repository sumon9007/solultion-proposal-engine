# Prompt: Refine Executive Summary

Use this prompt to rewrite or significantly improve an existing executive summary.

---

## Context Required

Before writing, confirm you have:
- Client name
- Their primary pain point
- The proposed solution type
- 2–3 key benefits we are delivering
- Any differentiators (why us, not a competitor)

If any of these are missing, ask before writing.

---

## Writing Framework

A great executive summary follows this structure:

**Paragraph 1 — The Client's Challenge**
Open with the client's name and problem. Be specific. Avoid generic openers. The first sentence must grab attention.

Example: "Acme Corporation faces a critical inflection point: their on-premises infrastructure, now seven years past its refresh cycle, is constraining growth and increasing operational risk."

**Paragraph 2 — Our Proposed Response**
State what we are proposing. Be decisive — use "we recommend" not "we would suggest" or "this proposal explores". Name the solution approach clearly.

**Paragraph 3 — Key Benefits**
List 2–3 specific, measurable, or time-bound benefits. Avoid vague claims.

Good: "This migration will reduce infrastructure operating costs by an estimated 35% and eliminate a 48-hour disaster recovery window currently exposing the business to material risk."
Bad: "This solution will improve efficiency and reduce costs while enabling digital transformation."

**Paragraph 4 — Confidence and Call to Action**
Close with a confident statement about our capability to deliver, and a forward-looking action. End with momentum, not hesitation.

---

## Tone Rules for Executive Summaries

- Write at C-suite reading level (no jargon, no acronyms without expansion)
- Maximum 200 words
- Active voice throughout
- No self-congratulatory phrases ("we are thrilled", "we are delighted")
- No hedging language ("we hope", "we believe", "it is anticipated")
- Specific over general — every sentence should earn its place

---

## Output

Produce the rewritten executive summary as a clean markdown block, ready to paste directly into the proposal.

After writing, ask: "Would you like me to adjust the tone, length, or emphasis of any part?"
