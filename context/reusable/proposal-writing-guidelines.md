# Proposal Writing Guidelines

These guidelines apply to all proposals produced in this workspace. They represent accumulated best practice for winning technology proposals.

---

## The Fundamental Rule

**Write for the decision-maker, not the technical reviewer.**

Most proposals are written by technical people who write for other technical people. The problem is that purchasing decisions are often made by non-technical executives — a CEO, CFO, or Operations Director — who care about business outcomes, not technical architecture.

Every section of a proposal must pass this test: *"Does a non-technical executive understand why this matters to them?"*

---

## Section-by-Section Guidance

### Executive Summary

The executive summary is the most important section. Many decision-makers read only this.

**Do:**
- Open with the client's problem — use their language, not ours
- Name the client explicitly in the first paragraph
- State what we propose in plain English by the end of the first paragraph
- Highlight 2–3 specific benefits that matter to this client (not generic ones)
- Close with a forward-looking, confident statement

**Don't:**
- Start with "We are pleased to present..." or any variant
- Use the executive summary to introduce your company (that's what "About Us" is for)
- Include technical details — those go in Proposed Solution
- Make more than 3–4 key points — less is more

---

### Understanding of Requirements

This section proves we listened. It should go beyond restating what the client told us — it should add our perspective.

**Do:**
- Show that we understand the business context, not just the technical problem
- Identify the risk of inaction (what happens if they do nothing?)
- Acknowledge constraints we know about (budget, timeline, legacy systems)
- Use bullet points for specific requirements

**Don't:**
- Simply copy the client's brief back to them
- Introduce our solution here — that's the next section
- Make assumptions without flagging them as assumptions

---

### Proposed Solution

This is where we earn our money. The solution section must be specific, justified, and client-relevant.

**Do:**
- Name specific technologies and products
- Explain why we chose this approach over alternatives
- Connect each element of the solution to a specific client requirement
- For cloud proposals: describe the architecture in plain language (no need for diagrams in drafts)
- For managed services: describe the service model clearly

**Don't:**
- List features without explaining client benefits
- Use vendor marketing language ("next-generation", "industry-leading")
- Assume the client knows what acronyms mean — expand every one on first use
- Write a generic solution that could apply to any client

---

### Scope of Work

The scope section is a commercial document as much as a technical one. Ambiguity here costs money and damages relationships.

**Do:**
- Be explicit about what is included in every phase
- Specify client responsibilities — "the client will provide X" not "X will be provided"
- Include a change control statement
- Include an in/out of scope table
- Specify effort estimates (person-days) alongside calendar duration

**Don't:**
- Use vague verbs: "support", "assist", "help with" — say what specifically we will do
- Leave dependencies implicit — state them
- Describe phases that overlap without explaining how they interleave

---

### Pricing

Pricing sections are read by finance teams. They must be clear, complete, and consistent with the scope.

**Do:**
- Use a table for every pricing scenario
- Include a "Total Investment" line in bold
- State VAT status and payment terms
- Offer optional phasing where the client may have budget constraints
- If pricing is indicative, say so explicitly and explain the qualification process

**Don't:**
- Present a lump sum with no breakdown
- Include line items that don't map to scope activities
- Be inconsistent (scope says 5 days, pricing shows 3)
- Leave out exclusions that affect cost (e.g., "third-party licences not included")

## Writing Quality Signals

### Signs of a strong proposal:
- Specific numbers: days, users, systems, percentages
- Client's name in the executive summary
- Explicit client responsibilities in scope
- Justification for technology choices
- Honest assumptions that protect scope

### Signs of a weak proposal:
- Generic executive summary that could apply to any client
- Vague scope language ("we will support your environment")
- Feature lists without client benefit translation
- No change control statement
- Pricing that doesn't match the scope

## Length and Formatting

| Section | Target Length |
|---------|-------------|
| Executive Summary | 100–200 words |
| Understanding of Requirements | 150–300 words |
| Proposed Solution | 200–500 words |
| Scope of Work | As detailed as needed |
| Deliverables | Table preferred |
| Assumptions | 5–12 bullet points |
| Exclusions | 5–15 bullet points |
| Pricing | Table |
| Timeline | Table |
| About Us | Standard template (do not customise) |
| Next Steps | Standard template (do not customise) |
| Legal Notes | Standard template (do not customise) |

---

## Final Check Before Sending

Before sending any proposal to a client, confirm:

- [ ] Executive summary opens with the client's problem
- [ ] Client name appears in the executive summary
- [ ] No placeholder text (`[TBD]`, `[INSERT]`, `TODO`) remains
- [ ] Pricing adds up correctly
- [ ] Pricing matches scope (no orphan line items)
- [ ] All sections are present in the correct order
- [ ] Validation script passes
- [ ] Proposal has been through the approval workflow
